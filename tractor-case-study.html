05 · Case Study · Tractor

Tractor CONNECT 2.0

Redesigning a connected tractor app for farmers who work with their hands, not their phones. A full end-to-end UX process, from field research to final screens, for Tractor's companion mobile application.

Our RoleLead UX Designer
PlatformiOS · Android
ClientTractor · Confidential client
MarketsUS · Canada · South Korea

The challenge

A powerful tractor ecosystem with a mobile app that couldn't match it. Complex fault codes, inaccessible maintenance tracking, and no clear path for users with low digital confidence.

Our approach

Full Design Thinking process: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. Every design decision traceable to a research finding.

The outcome

Tractor Connect 2.0, a field-ready companion built for the way farmers actually think and work, not how engineers assume they do.

Discovery to prototype delivery.

00 · Executive Summary

A field-ready companion. Built for Robert, Sarah, and Carlos.

We led the end-to-end redesign of the Connected Tractor App, a companion product that lets owners monitor equipment health, manage maintenance, schedule service, access parts, and read telemetry data through a single mobile interface. The goal was to modernise an experience that had fallen behind the hardware it represents: a capable tractor ecosystem served by an app that couldn't communicate any of it clearly.

The product spans three markets (the US, Canada, and South Korea) and a deliberately wide user base: independent farmers with low digital confidence, fleet managers running multi-vehicle operations, and dealer service technicians who live in the app all day. Designing one coherent system for three incompatible mental models became the central problem of the project.

Our role

Lead UX Designer. We owned the complete end-to-end process: research strategy, user definition, information architecture, journey mapping, task flows, ideation, wireframing, the design system, UI design, and prototype documentation.

Team structure

We collaborated with product stakeholders, engineering leads, and dealer representatives throughout the project, while independently leading all UX deliverables. For the user interaction research, we brought in an independent UX research consultant based in the United States to add an outside perspective to interview design and synthesis.

01 · Discovery — Empathize

Understanding the machine. Then the person behind it.

Stakeholder workshops

We opened the project with alignment sessions across Tractor's product and engineering teams. Before touching a screen, we needed the business goals stated plainly: digital service bookings that drive dealer revenue, parts orders completed in-app, and a registration funnel that doesn't leak new owners. We mapped each goal to a measurable signal so that later design decisions could be defended against intent, not taste.

Those same sessions surfaced the hard constraints. The app sits on top of a TCU/TMS telemetry architecture with real offline requirements (the field is frequently out of signal) and must localise cleanly across three markets. We documented these as non-negotiable design inputs: every data-dependent screen would have to behave gracefully when the network, the hardware, or the language changed underneath it.

Existing product audit

We ran a full audit of the shipping app, walking every flow and scoring it against Nielsen's ten usability heuristics. The most damaging pattern was structural: five separate features collapsed into the same generic error state whenever the TCU wasn't active, a dead end repeated five times, with no explanation and no way forward. A user who hit it once had no reason to believe any of those features worked.

The audit also exposed competing home-dashboard variants shipping side by side, and navigation patterns that changed meaning between sections. The information hierarchy gave six features equal weight, so nothing told the user what mattered right now. We catalogued each issue with a screenshot, a heuristic reference, and the specific user cost: the evidence base the redesign would have to answer to.

Competitive analysis

We benchmarked the category leaders (John Deere's JDLink, Kubota's MyKubota, Mahindra, and New Holland), focusing on the three areas where Tractor was weakest. How does each handle offline states? How does each communicate maintenance intervals? And how does each present fault codes to a non-technical owner? The pattern was clear: competitors who translated machine data into human language earned trust, and those who exposed raw codes and engine-hour math did not. Tractor's opportunity wasn't more features, it was clearer communication of the data it already had.

Key opportunities

Fault communication gap

No plain-language fault explanations exist across any Tractor screen, only raw codes.

Maintenance literacy gap

Engine hours are used as the primary interval metric, inaccessible to non-technical users.

Post-registration gap

No clear value is delivered in the first 60 seconds after tractor setup.

We conducted interviews with individual tractor owners, fleet managers, and dealer service technicians to understand daily workflows, pain points, and decision-making patterns.

Key research findings

1

Average US farmer age is 58.1 years.

The primary user base has significantly lower digital confidence than a typical app's target audience. Every design decision must account for users who are not digital natives. (Source: USDA Census of Agriculture, 2022)

2

Fault codes create disproportionate anxiety.

When a warning light appeared, users had no context for severity. The emotional response was panic, regardless of whether the fault was minor or critical, because the app provided no plain-language interpretation.

3

Engine hours mean nothing to someone who thinks in seasons.

Maintenance intervals expressed in engine hours (50hr, 100hr, 200hr) were not understood by the majority of users. Farmers think in seasons, jobs, and calendar months, not accumulated run time.

4

Compatibility anxiety kills parts orders.

Users added parts to the cart, then called the dealer to confirm compatibility before completing the purchase. This happened consistently across interviews. The uncertainty was never resolved by the app itself.

5

Rural connectivity is not reliable.

Critical features (fault history, maintenance records, dealer contacts) must function without a network connection. The field is where these features are needed most.

6

Post-registration is the "what now?" moment.

If the app fails to deliver obvious value within 60 seconds of tractor registration, users do not return to explore further. First-session value is the strongest predictor of long-term engagement.

7

The dealer relationship is an asset, not a competitor.

Farmers trust their dealer. The instinct to make the app replace dealer contact is wrong: the opportunity is to strengthen and pre-fill that connection.

In their words

When that light comes on, I stop everything. I don't know if it means I'm about to lose the engine or if it's nothing. The app just shows me a code.

I've been farming for 30 years. I don't think in hours. I think in whether I need to get this done before the planting starts.

I always call Ride North before I order parts. I'm not going to guess and waste money on the wrong filter.

03 · Define — Define

From 7 findings to 5 solvable problems.

12.7%Older farmers confident with digital tools (Purdue University).

Problem statements

Plain-language faults. Farmers with low digital literacy need fault codes explained in plain language and severity context, because the current experience gives them raw codes with no guidance, causing disproportionate anxiety that stops work unnecessarily.

Human maintenance intervals. Tractor owners need maintenance intervals communicated in seasonal, human language, because engine-hour intervals are technically accurate but practically meaningless to the farmers who need to act on them.

Compatibility before commitment. Users ordering parts online need compatibility confirmed before committing to a purchase, because the current experience forces them to verify through a separate dealer call, creating friction that consistently kills online orders.

Immediate post-registration value. New users need immediate, visible value after tractor registration, because the current experience provides no clear next step, and first-session abandonment is the primary driver of long-term disengagement.

Jobs to be Done

When a warning light appears, I want to understand what it means and what to do about it, so I can make an informed decision about whether to stop work or continue.

When my tractor approaches a service interval, I want to be reminded in language I understand, so I can schedule service before it becomes overdue.

When I need to order a replacement part, I want to confirm it fits my specific tractor before committing, so I can order confidently without calling the dealer.

06 · Information Architecture — Define

One structure. Three completely different users served.

We moved the app from a cluttered three-tab navigation to a clear four-tab structure (Home, My Tractor, Explore, Profile) with progressive disclosure as the core organising principle. Simplicity on the surface, depth on demand: one architecture that serves an anxious owner, a fleet manager, and a technician without compromising any of them.

Key IA decisions

1

4-tab navigation

We separated vehicle functions (My Tractor) from discovery (Explore) so Robert never accidentally leaves his tractor context when browsing parts.

2

Ask Tractor as a persistent float

The AI assistant is accessible from every screen without consuming a navigation tab. Available, but never imposing.

3

Progressive disclosure

A simple surface for Robert, depth on demand for Sarah, speed shortcuts for Carlos, one structure serving three distinct usage patterns.

4

TCU-aware states

Every data-dependent screen is designed for three TCU states (active, not enabled, expired) so no feature ever becomes a dead end.

5 winning concepts

1

The Status Card

Problem. The home screen showed six equal tiles with no priority signal. Nothing told Robert what mattered right now.

Solution. A single dynamic card leading with the most important status at that moment: fault active, maintenance due, or all clear. One message, one CTA, zero ambiguity.

Principle · One thing, said clearly.

2

The Severity System

Problem. All fault codes felt equally alarming. Anxiety was identical whether it was a loose cap or a critical engine failure.

Solution. A 3-tier classification (Low / Medium / Critical) with distinct plain-English copy and severity-matched CTAs. The design communicates urgency before a word is read.

Principle · The right amount of urgency.

3

The Interval Track

Problem. Engine-hour intervals were technically accurate but practically meaningless to farmers who think in seasons.

Solution. A visual progress track that speaks in plain language: "Oil change due before spring planting season." The math is done. The user just acts.

Principle · Speak the farmer's language, not the machine's.

4

The Dealer Handoff Pattern

Problem. Every flow ending in dealer contact started from scratch. Robert had to explain his model, the fault, his service history, everything the app already knew.

Solution. Every dealer CTA pre-populates with full context: vehicle model, fault code, last service, contact preference. The dealer receives the complete picture before they respond.

Principle · The app does the explaining.

5

The Parts Certainty Badge

Problem. Compatibility anxiety killed parts orders. Users always called the dealer to confirm before completing an online purchase.

Solution. A green "Perfect fit for your TX-900" badge appearing before the Add to Cart button, confirmed at the exact moment of decision.

Principle · Confidence before commitment.

09 · Wireframing — Design

Structure before style. Decisions before aesthetics.

We started with lo-fi structural sketches to settle layout and hierarchy questions while they were still cheap to change, then moved to mid-fi wireframes carrying real content and component shapes, resolving interaction detail last. Nailing structure before style keeps aesthetic debate from masquerading as design progress.

→ Drag through lo-fi → mid-fi

Explore the screens · lo-fi ↔ mid-fi

Five live wireframes, one per winning concept. Toggle between the lo-fi skeleton and the mid-fi layout to see how structure firmed up into real content.

Key wireframe decisions

The status card occupies the top third of home, forcing a priority-hierarchy decision on every visit rather than giving equal weight to every feature.

The severity badge is the first element in the fault screen, not the fault name. Colour communicates urgency before language does.

The interval track uses a progress bar as the primary element, not a number. Visual position communicates urgency faster than "50 hrs" to someone who thinks in seasons.

The dealer handoff screen has one primary action: confirm and move forward. All context is pre-filled. This is confirmation, not a form.

Two iterations mattered most between lo-fi and mid-fi. Internal design review showed the fault name reading first and undercutting the colour signal, so we moved the severity badge above the fault name, letting urgency register pre-linguistically. And the parts compatibility note, originally inline text, was easy to miss at the decision point, so we promoted it to a distinct green badge component sitting directly above Add to Cart.

B · Accessibility Standards

Contrast

All text meets WCAG AA contrast minimums (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text). Status colours (green / amber / red) are never the sole carrier of meaning — every instance pairs colour with a text label or icon.

Finding 5

Touch targets

44×44px minimum, 48×48px for primary actions — directly responding to gloved-hand field use.

Contextual inquiry · 7 of 12

Outdoor legibility

Body text minimum 14px, high-contrast surface colours chosen for visibility in direct sunlight — validated against the finding that 7 of 12 interview sessions involved outdoor screen use in direct light.

Finding 1

Plain language

All system messaging, error states and status communication is written at a reading level appropriate for the lowest digital-confidence segment of the user base (12.7% digital confidence per Finding 1), not the most technically fluent.

C · Usage Principles

  1. Orange is earned — reserved for the single most important action on any given screen, never decorative.

  2. Colour speaks before text — status colours communicate state at a glance, but never alone, always paired with a label.

  3. Oswald for numbers and labels, DM Sans for everything a user actually reads — the technical font never carries a full sentence.

  4. Every component assumes gloved hands, bright sunlight, and a user who may be checking this screen for the first time today.

13 · Outcomes — Test

What changed. How we measured it.

88%Task completion across the core flows — target >85%. ✓
74sAverage fault-alert → action — target <90s. ✓
78%Parts orders completed without a dealer call — target >70%. ✓
81System Usability Scale score — target >75. ✓

All four primary success metrics were met or exceeded across 9 moderated sessions — five tractor owners, two fleet managers, and two dealer technicians. Each target was defined with stakeholders at the start and tied to both a business goal (dealer revenue through digital booking and parts) and a user goal (confidence, speed, independence), so the gains can be read honestly rather than assumed.

Three changes testing forced

1

A louder RDI signature step

Two of nine participants walked past the signature field in the RDI checklist. We added a stronger visual prompt before the field so the step can't be skipped.

2

A higher-contrast amber

The medium-severity badge read poorly for a participant with colour-vision deficiency. We raised the amber value and re-verified it against WCAG AA.

3

Plainer words

"Mark complete" tripped one participant. We changed it to "Mark as done" — small, but exactly the plain-language discipline the whole product is built on.

14 · Reflection

What we learned. What we would do differently.

1

Research for someone very different from yourself

The USDA statistic (average farmer age 58.1) changed every design decision. Without it, we would have designed for a digitally confident user who doesn't exist in this market. The lesson: proxy your assumptions with data, or your empathy is just projection.

2

The dealer is not the enemy

The instinct was to make the app remove the need for dealer calls. The research showed the opposite: farmers trust their dealer, and that trust is a feature, not a problem. The best UX decision in the project was the Dealer Handoff Pattern, using the app to strengthen that relationship, not compete with it.

3

Progressive disclosure is a research decision

Surfacing simplicity first and depth on demand wasn't an aesthetic preference, it came from the tension between three distinct user segments with incompatible needs. One IA. Three completely different ways of using it. That's only possible when the structure is grounded in actual behavioural research.

4

The next phase is the field, not the file

Moderated testing with 9 participants hit all four targets and forced three real fixes — but a prototype tested remotely is not a product proven across a Saskatchewan winter or a planting-season rush. The honest next step is instrumented field testing across all three markets and connectivity states, measuring the same four metrics in production.
