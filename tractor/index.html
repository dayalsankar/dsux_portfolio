Redesigning a connected-tractor companion app for farmers who work with their hands, not their phones. A full end-to-end UX process — from field research to final screens — for a tractor manufacturer's mobile product, built around how farmers actually think and work, not how engineers assume they do.

00 · Executive Summary

A field-ready companion — built for Robert, Sarah, and Carlos.

We led the end-to-end redesign of a connected-tractor companion app — a product that lets owners monitor equipment health, manage maintenance, schedule service, order parts, and read telemetry through a single mobile interface. The goal was to modernise an experience that had fallen behind the hardware it represents: a capable tractor ecosystem served by an app that couldn't communicate any of it clearly.

The challenge — The product spans three markets and a deliberately wide user base — independent farmers with low digital confidence, fleet managers running multi-vehicle operations, and dealer technicians who live in the app all day. Designing one coherent system for three incompatible mental models became the central problem of the project.

The outcome — Tractor Connect 2.0 — a field-ready companion in which every design decision traces back to a research finding. Across nine moderated test sessions, all four primary success metrics were met or exceeded: 88% task completion, 74-second fault-to-action, 78% of parts orders placed without a dealer call, and a System Usability Scale score of 81.

01 — The dashboard light

It starts in a field. A warning light appears on the dash, and the only thing the app offers is a raw code — SPN 1234 — with no hint of whether the engine is about to fail or it's nothing at all.

02 — Capable machine, mute app

The tractor itself is a sophisticated, connected piece of equipment. The app meant to represent it exposed engine-hour math and fault codes, then went silent the moment the network dropped.

03 — The user is 58, not 28

The average US farmer is 58 years old, with significantly lower digital confidence than a typical app's audience. This product wasn't failing power users — it was failing the people it was built for.

04 — Translate, don't add

The opportunity was never more features. It was clearer communication of the data the tractor already produced — turning machine language into human language, at the exact moment a decision has to be made.

01 · The Field

The world the app actually lives in.

The product lives in a specific world: rural fields with unreliable connectivity, gloved hands, direct sunlight, and users who think in seasons and jobs — not engine hours and error codes.

Farmers think in seasons and jobs. The app spoke in codes.

Three markets, three languages, and one telemetry architecture (TCU/TMS) that is frequently out of signal. A digital system here can't assume a confident, always-connected user at a desk. It has to formalise how farmers already work — and stay useful when the network, the hardware, or the language changes underneath it.

02 · Problem Statement

The app wasn't missing features.
It was speaking the wrong language.

The biggest problem wasn't any single feature being difficult. It was that the app spoke the machine's language — codes, engine hours, raw telemetry — to people who needed answers, not data.

01

Fault codes with no meaning

When a warning light appeared, users got a raw code and no context for severity. The emotional response was panic — regardless of whether the fault was trivial or critical — because nothing translated it.

02

Five identical dead ends

Five separate features collapsed into the same generic error whenever the TCU wasn't active — one dead end repeated five times, with no explanation and no way forward. Hit it once and you'd assume none of it worked.

03

Engine hours mean nothing

Maintenance intervals were expressed in engine hours (50hr, 100hr, 200hr). Farmers who think in seasons, jobs, and calendar months couldn't act on a number that didn't map to their world.

04

Compatibility anxiety kills orders

Users added parts to the cart, then called the dealer to confirm the part would fit before buying. The uncertainty the app created was never resolved by the app itself — so the order died on the phone.

03 · Research

Ten methods. Seven findings. Every decision traceable.

With a wide, hard-to-reach user base, ten research methods were triangulated to reconstruct how farmers, fleet managers, and technicians actually work — and where the current experience breaks. An independent US-based UX research consultant joined for interview design and synthesis.

Seven findings, one direction

F1

The average US farmer is 58.1 years old.

The primary user base has significantly lower digital confidence than a typical app's audience. Every decision had to account for users who are not digital natives. (USDA Census of Agriculture, 2022.)

F2

Fault codes create disproportionate anxiety.

With no plain-language interpretation, a warning light produced panic regardless of severity. Users had no way to tell a minor alert from an engine-ending one.

F3

Engine hours mean nothing to someone who thinks in seasons.

Intervals in 50hr / 100hr / 200hr terms weren't understood by most users. Farmers think in jobs, seasons, and calendar months — not accumulated run-time.

F4

Compatibility anxiety kills parts orders.

Users consistently added a part to the cart, then called the dealer to confirm fit before buying. The app created the doubt and never resolved it.

F5

Rural connectivity is not reliable.

Fault history, maintenance records, and dealer contacts must work without a network — the field is exactly where these features are needed most.

F6

Post-registration is the 'what now?' moment.

If the app fails to deliver obvious value within 60 seconds of registration, users don't return. First-session value is the strongest predictor of long-term engagement.

F7

The dealer is an asset, not a competitor.

Farmers trust their dealer. The instinct to make the app replace dealer contact is wrong — the opportunity is to strengthen and pre-fill that connection.

08 · Design Principles

Five rules that filtered every decision.

01

Translate, never expose

Machine data — fault codes, engine hours, telemetry — is translated into plain, human language at the moment of decision. The system carries the meaning, not the raw value.

Traces to F2 · F3 — codes and hours mean nothing
02

Severity before words

Colour and icon communicate how urgent something is before the user reads a single sentence — and colour is never the only signal, always paired with a label.

Traces to F2 — fault anxiety, and WCAG contrast
03

Never a dead end

Every data-dependent screen is designed for all three TCU states — active, not enabled, expired — so no feature ever collapses into a generic error with no way forward.

Traces to Audit — five identical dead ends
04

Field-first, offline-safe

Critical features work without a network, in direct sunlight, with gloves on. The app assumes the hardest conditions are the normal ones.

Traces to F5 · contextual inquiry
05

Strengthen the dealer, don't replace them

Farmers trust their dealer. Every handoff is pre-filled with vehicle, fault, and history so the relationship starts from what the app already knows.

Traces to F7 — the dealer is an asset

09 · Information Architecture

One structure. Three completely different users served.

We moved the app from a cluttered three-tab navigation to a clear four-tab structure — Home, My Tractor, Explore, Profile — with progressive disclosure as the core organising principle. Simplicity on the surface, depth on demand: one architecture that serves an anxious owner, a fleet manager, and a technician without compromising any of them.

1

4-tab navigation

Vehicle functions (My Tractor) are separated from discovery (Explore) so Robert never accidentally leaves his tractor context while browsing parts.

2

Ask Tractor as a persistent float

The AI assistant is reachable from every screen without consuming a navigation tab. Available, but never imposing.

3

Progressive disclosure

A simple surface for Robert, depth on demand for Sarah, speed shortcuts for Carlos — one structure serving three usage patterns.

4

TCU-aware states

Every data-dependent screen is designed for three TCU states, so no feature ever becomes a dead end.

Progressive disclosure wasn't an aesthetic preference — it came from the tension between three distinct segments with incompatible needs. One IA, three completely different ways of using it.

11 · Major Design Decisions

Five decisions that shaped the build.

Each one with the challenge, the alternatives considered, why this path won, the impact, and the lesson. Expand to read the full reasoning.

Challenge

A warning light produced panic because the app showed only a raw code with no severity context.

The decision

Pair every fault with a one-sentence plain-language explanation, a colour-coded severity, and a single matched next step.

Alternatives

Keep the code and add a 'learn more' link to a manual.

Why this won

Anxiety comes from not knowing how bad it is. Severity-before-words removes the panic before the reading even starts.

Impact

Average fault-to-action of 74 seconds in testing, against a 90-second target.

Lesson

For anxious users, the first job of the interface is to lower the heart rate.

12 · Feature Deep-Dive

Six features, every one answering a finding.

From fault translation to offline mode — each feature exists to solve a specific, evidenced failure of the old experience, with the UX reasoning made explicit.

Fault Translation

Every fault code becomes a plain-language explanation with colour-coded severity and one matched next step — keep working, monitor, or book the dealer.

Why · Removes the panic response to a warning light (F2).

Seasonal Maintenance

Service intervals spoken in seasons and jobs — 'before spring planting' — with engine hours kept underneath for anyone who wants the raw number.

Why · Reminders only work if the user can act on the unit (F3).

Parts Compatibility

Browsing is filtered to the user's exact tractor, and a compatibility badge confirms fit before Add to Cart — answering the doubt where it's created.

Why · Compatibility anxiety is what kills online parts orders (F4).

Dealer Handoff

Service requests arrive at the dealer pre-filled with model, fault, and history, so the trusted relationship starts from what the app already knows.

Why · The dealer is an asset to strengthen, not replace (F7).

Health Monitoring

Battery voltage, coolant temperature, and brake efficiency surfaced as plain status pills — Good, Due, Critical — not a wall of telemetry.

Why · Visibility at a glance, designed for all three TCU states.

Offline Mode

Fault history, maintenance records, and dealer contacts are cached and readable with no signal, with a clear, jargon-free sync state when connectivity returns.

Why · The field is where these features are needed most (F5).

Answers, not codes.

Ask Tractor is the one AI surface in the product, with one job: turn a worried question into a plain answer, from any screen, without replacing the dealer the user trusts.

Answers, not codes

Natural-language, image-supported responses to 'what does this light mean?' — the help system the heuristic audit found missing.

Available, never imposing

A persistent float reachable from every screen — present when needed, but it never costs a navigation tab.

Every answer is written for the lowest digital-confidence segment of the user base, not the most technically fluent.

15 · Accessibility & Ethics

Designed for gloves, glare, and low digital confidence.

A field tool for a multi-generational, multilingual, low-digital-confidence user base is an accessibility problem as much as a usability one. These were requirements, not finishing touches.

Contrast, AA minimum

All text meets WCAG AA contrast (4.5:1 body, 3:1 large). Status colours are never the sole carrier of meaning — every instance pairs colour with a label or icon.

Touch targets for gloves

44×44px minimum, raised to 48×48px for primary actions used in the field — directly answering gloved-hand interaction, which standard mobile guidelines under-serve.

Legible in sunlight

Body text never below 14px, on high-contrast surfaces chosen for direct light — validated against the finding that 7 of 12 sessions involved outdoor screen use.

Plain language as policy

Every message, error, and status is written at a reading level set by the least confident user (the 12.7%), not the most fluent.

18 · Usability Testing

Nine participants. Six tasks. Three real fixes.

Validation was moderated remote testing on a Figma prototype: nine participants across all three user types, six core tasks, sixty minutes each. Success criteria were agreed with stakeholders up front and tied to both a business goal and a user goal, so the gains could be read honestly rather than assumed.

The test plan

MethodModerated remote usability testing
Participants5 owners · 2 fleet managers · 2 technicians
PlatformFigma prototype on a mobile device
Sessions60 minutes per participant
Tasks6 core tasks across the 5 winning concepts
What testing forced
  • A louder RDI signature step — 2 of 9 participants walked past the field; a stronger visual prompt now precedes it.
  • A higher-contrast amber — the medium-severity badge read poorly for a participant with colour-vision deficiency; raised and re-verified against WCAG AA.
  • Plainer words — 'Mark complete' tripped a participant; changed to 'Mark as done', the same plain-language discipline the product is built on.

Small changes, but exactly the plain-language and accessibility discipline the whole product is built on.

20 · Reflection

What we learned. What we'd do differently.

01

Research for someone very different from yourself.

The USDA statistic — average farmer age 58.1 — changed every design decision. Without it, we'd have designed for a digitally confident user who doesn't exist in this market. Proxy your assumptions with data, or your empathy is just projection.

02

The dealer is not the enemy.

The instinct was to make the app remove dealer calls. Research showed the opposite: farmers trust their dealer, and that trust is a feature. The best decision in the project — the Dealer Handoff Pattern — used the app to strengthen that relationship, not compete with it.

03

Progressive disclosure is a research decision.

Simplicity first, depth on demand wasn't an aesthetic preference — it resolved the tension between three segments with incompatible needs. One IA, three ways of using it. That's only possible when the structure is grounded in real behavioural research.

04

The next phase is the field, not the file.

Nine moderated sessions hit all four targets and forced three real fixes — but a prototype tested remotely is not a product proven across a Saskatchewan winter or a planting-season rush. The honest next step is instrumented field testing across all three markets and connectivity states.

21 · Future Roadmap

The next phase is the field, not the file.

01

Instrumented field testing

Measure the same four metrics in production across all three markets and connectivity states — the planting-season rush and the winter field, not the lab.

02

Full Korean localisation & RTL-aware layouts

South Korea is a launch market; critical operational screens need first-class localisation, not a translation layer bolted on.

03

Fleet intelligence for Sarah

Multi-vehicle analytics, demand forecasting for peak season, and proactive maintenance planning built on real telemetry history.

04

Deeper offline sync

Expand cached, write-capable offline flows so more than reads survive a day with no signal — and sync transparently when it returns.

05

Dealer-side companion

A technician view that mirrors the customer's pre-filled context, closing the Carlos loop end to end.

06

Ask Tractor, expanded

Grow the assistant's coverage with image-based fault recognition and guided, step-by-step field fixes.

Final Takeaways

Tractor Connect 2.0 didn't need more features. It needed to translate a capable machine into language the people who depend on it could actually use — and to stay useful in the field, on the hardest day, with gloves on and no signal.
