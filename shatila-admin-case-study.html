Shatila Bakery Factory · Admin Dashboard · UX/UI Case Study

When a 40-year-old factory runs on memory alone.

Shatila Bakery has operated in Dearborn, Michigan since 1979 — producing Lebanese and Middle Eastern sweets for one of the largest Arab-American communities in the United States. Every order, every production instruction, every delivery: managed entirely through phone calls, paper notes, and the memory of a small, trusted team. This is the case study of designing their first digital system.

00 · Project Summary

A factory's first digital operating system.

Shatila is not a small bakery. It is a custom-order sweets factory — producing large quantities of baklava, maamoul, knafeh, and traditional pastries to order for direct customers across Michigan and beyond. Their customers come for weddings, religious celebrations, community events, and institutional supply. Every order is different. Every order is personal. And until this project, every order was managed entirely without a screen.

01 · Discovery — Empathize

What the client told me directly.

I had multiple conversations with the Shatila stakeholder across the project. These weren't formal research sessions — they were working conversations, happening alongside design. But they were the most important research I did, because they gave me the real operational picture: not what the business looked like from the outside, but how it actually worked inside.

The Urgency Context

The stakeholder came to me during their Christmas peak season. They were not looking for a long research engagement or a phased roadmap. They needed the admin dashboard — specifically, management visibility into all active orders — as a priority. Christmas was approaching. Orders were already falling through the cracks. The system that had worked reliably for decades was breaking under seasonal volume. That urgency shaped every scope decision I made.

Four Pain Points — Heard Directly
01

Orders Getting Lost

During peak season, orders taken by phone fell through the gap between being recorded and being acted on. A busy day meant some orders simply never made it to the production team.

02

No Visibility Across the Operation

Management had no way to see the status of all active orders simultaneously. The only method available was to physically walk to each team and ask. During Christmas volume, that was impossible.

03

Manual Process Too Slow for Peak Volume

The system that reliably handled 30 orders a week broke down under 100. Not because the team worked differently — because the process had no capacity to scale.

04

Details Getting Wrong Between Staff

Custom specifications — specific quantities, flavour variations, packaging requirements, delivery instructions — were passed verbally. By the time they reached the baker, critical details were missing, assumed, or wrong.

What I Understood Before Opening Figma

The primary user is management first. The person who needs to see all orders, all statuses, and catch problems before they become customer complaints.

The biggest problem is not any individual task being difficult — it is nobody knowing what state the operation is in at any given moment.

Custom order specifications must be preserved completely. A system that loses specifics is worse than no system at all.

Speed matters more than completeness at first launch. A simple system the team will use beats a comprehensive system that slows them down.

This system has to perform during Christmas. The highest-stress, highest-volume, most failure-prone time of year for this business.

03 · Define

Six findings. One direction.

F1F1

Information visibility is the core problem, not any individual task.

Each team does their job competently. What fails is the gap between stages — when nobody knows what the previous team finished or what the next team needs.

F2F2

Custom specifications degrade with every verbal retelling.

Research on bespoke manufacturing shows specifications lose 30–40% of detail through two or more verbal handoffs. For a custom bakery order, that means wrong products baked and customer callbacks.

F3F3

Peak season is the real test.

A system that works on a quiet Tuesday but fails on December 23rd is not a solution. Every design decision was filtered through: does this work when the team is at maximum stress and minimum patience?

F4F4

Management needs overview before anything else.

The stakeholder's first priority was visibility — seeing all orders and their status simultaneously. That drove the dashboard-first, admin-first design decision.

F5F5

The team trusts their existing behaviour.

Staff call customers to verify orders. That behaviour won't disappear because a dashboard exists. The design had to support it, not fight it.

F6F6

The existing system isn't broken — it is unscalable.

A manual workflow that has operated for 40 years has real strengths. The design must formalise what works, not replace it with something foreign.

How Might We
VisibilityHow might we give management a single view of all active orders without requiring manual status updates from each team?
VisibilityHow might we surface the most urgent orders without the admin having to search for them?
CommunicationHow might we ensure custom specifications travel intact from the customer's phone call to the baker's hands?
CommunicationHow might we notify the next team automatically when the previous stage completes?
CommunicationHow might we make the customer verification call faster and more complete for tele-callers?
AdoptionHow might we design a system simpler than the paper process it replaces — for every individual role?
AdoptionHow might we earn the team's trust in the digital system before they need to rely on it during peak season?

07 · Key User Flows

Four flows that carry the operation.

Flow 1 — New Order Creation

The primary path: turning a customer phone call into a confirmed, tracked order.

Open Order Entry→Select Customer→Review Customer Issues→Enter Order Items→Set Shipping Address→Set Payment Terms→Review Order Summary→Confirm + Submit→Order Confirmed
Flow 2 — Customer Creation with AI Auto-Fill

Cutting data entry time by extracting structured fields from raw text.

New Customer→Open AI Auto-Fill Modal→Paste customer text or email→System extracts fields→Review extracted data→Correct if needed→Save customer→Customer ready for orders
Flow 3 — Fulfillment to Shipment

The hand-off path from picking to a tracked shipment.

Open Fulfillment Queue→Select Order→Review Pick List→Mark Items Picked→Generate Shipping Label→Hand to Shipping Team→Mark Shipped→Tracking Active
Flow 4 — Accounts Receivable

Closing the loop from delivered order to recorded payment.

Order Delivered→Invoice Auto-Created→Review in AR Module→Send Invoice to Customer→Monitor Payment Status→Record Payment Received→AR Aging Updated
08 · Ideation — Define + Ideate

Principles before pixels.

Four Design Principles
01
Visibility Before Action

The system's most important job is making information visible. Before anyone acts on an order, they must be able to see it clearly, completely, and without searching.

Traces to F1: information visibility is the core problem

02
Preserve the Original

Custom specifications must travel from the customer's words to the baker's hands without being filtered, summarised, or lost. The system carries the original — not an interpretation.

Traces to F2: specifications degrade with every handoff

03
Support Existing Behaviour

The tele-caller verification call has worked for 40 years. Design the system to make that call better — not to eliminate it. Earn trust by improving what the team already trusts.

Traces to F5: the team trusts existing behaviour

04
Simple Enough for Christmas

Every interaction must work when the team is at maximum stress, maximum volume, and minimum patience. If a feature needs explanation, it will not be used on December 23rd.

Traces to F3: peak season is the real test

What Comes Next — 5 Priorities
1

Role-based home screens

Each of the 5 roles should see a different home screen tailored to their daily tasks. Warehouse Staff don't need AR aging. Admin don't need only a pick list.

2

Production queue for the baking team

The bakers have no screen in the current design. Their workflow — what to bake today, in what quantity, with what specification — needs its own dedicated interface.

3

Automatic stage handoff notifications

When fulfillment marks an order complete, shipping should be notified instantly. The digital version of the verbal handoff needs to be built.

4

Mobile and tablet view

Factory floor workers are not at desks. The fulfillment and shipping interfaces need to be touch-friendly and readable in a production environment.

5

Bilingual UI — English and Arabic

Dearborn's Arab-American workforce means Arabic is a working language for many team members. Critical operational screens should be bilingual.

13 · Reflection

What I learned.

Design for the urgency, not the ideal.

The client came with a real deadline — Christmas. The right decision was to solve their most urgent problem first: management visibility over all active orders. A useful tool delivered in time is worth more than a perfect tool delivered after the problem has already caused damage. Scope decisions made under real constraints are still design decisions — and they need to be owned, not apologised for.

Support existing behaviour before trying to change it.

The tele-caller verification call has worked for 40 years. Designing the Order Summary Review to make that call better — giving staff the complete order in front of them while they're on the phone — gets adoption. Designing a system that tries to eliminate the call gets resistance. For a first-time digital tool, the fastest path to trust is making the existing process undeniably better, not replacing it.

Role-based design starts with permissions, not screens.

I built the 5-role permission system into the foundation before any screen used it. That means the next phase — differentiated home screens per role — has real infrastructure to build on rather than needing to be retrofitted. Good design anticipates the decision it cannot make yet and creates the conditions for it.

First-time digitalisation is a different problem from redesign.

There is no prior system to improve, no analytics to review, no existing mental model to respect. The only reference is the people and how they currently work. Listening to what the client describes — not just what they ask for — is the entire research method. The four pain points they raised in our conversations shaped every structural decision in this design.
