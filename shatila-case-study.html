04 · Case Study · Shatila

Shatila BAKERY FACTORY

A staff operations dashboard for a 40-year-old Lebanese-American sweets factory in Dearborn, Michigan — moving from phone calls and paper to its first digital system. An end-to-end UX/UI case study.

RoleUI/UX Designer
ToolsFigma Make · React
StatusActive Project
LocationDearborn, Michigan
00 · Overview

A craft factory, run for 40 years without a screen.

Shatila is a Lebanese-American sweets factory based in Dearborn, Michigan, specialising in custom, large-quantity dessert orders for events, retailers and wholesale clients. Family-run and operating for more than 40 years, the factory built its reputation on craft and consistency.

But its order operations still run entirely on phone calls and paper. This case study covers the design of the first digital system to support the factory's day-to-day staff workflow — capturing orders, tracking their status, and giving every team one shared source of truth.

Key findings

F1

Phone-first intake

Nearly every order begins as a phone call, captured on paper and never entered into any shared system.

F2

Status lives in people's heads

The true state of an order is only known by whoever last touched it, making handoffs fragile and progress invisible.

F3

Peak season breaks the process

The manual flow that works in normal months collapses under Christmas-level order volume.

F4

Custom specs are high-risk

The more bespoke an order, the more detail is lost as it moves verbally between sales, warehouse and production.

F5

No single source of truth

Each team keeps its own informal records, none of which agree with the others.

F6

Real appetite for change

Staff are frustrated with the current process and ready to adopt a system that genuinely reduces their workload.

05 · Design Decisions — Design

Five decisions that shaped the build.

01Staff dashboard before admin
What happened
Most daily pain lived with front-line staff capturing and moving orders.
The decision
Design the staff dashboard first, before the admin / management layer.
What this means
The system delivers value where the work actually happens, fastest.
02Order Summary Review as a tele-caller tool
What happened
Sales staff needed to confirm complex custom orders mid-call.
The decision
Build an Order Summary Review screen tailored to the tele-caller flow.
What this means
Fewer mistakes confirmed at the source, before the order moves on.
03Customer issues before order creation
What happened
Repeat customers often called about existing problems, not new orders.
The decision
Surface customer issues earlier in the flow, ahead of new order creation.
What this means
Staff resolve the real reason for the call instead of forcing a new order.
04AI Auto-Fill for data entry
What happened
Manual entry was the single biggest drag on intake speed.
The decision
Introduce AI auto-fill to pre-populate fields from existing data.
What this means
Staff confirm rather than type, cutting time and entry errors.
05Role-based permissions in the foundation
What happened
Different teams need different access to the same order data.
The decision
Bake role-based permissions into the foundation, not bolt them on later.
What this means
The system scales to admin and production roles without a rebuild.

08 · Key Learnings — Reflection

What this project taught me.

Start where the pain is, not where the org chart is.

Designing the staff dashboard before the admin layer meant the people doing the most repetitive, error-prone work felt the benefit first — giving the project early momentum and buy-in.

When there's no system, conversations are the data.

With no documentation or software to audit, structured client conversations became the entire research foundation. Reconstructing the workflow by listening carefully beat any template.

Speed of entry is a design problem, not a willpower one.

Staff weren't slow — the process was. Treating fast, structured intake as a core constraint reframed AI auto-fill and order review from nice-to-haves into the heart of the solution.

Foundations decide what's possible later.

Building role-based permissions in early kept the door open for admin, production and mobile phases without forcing a costly rebuild down the line.
