00 · Executive Summary

A factory's first digital operating system.

Shatila is not a small bakery. It is a custom-order sweets factory — producing large quantities of baklava, maamoul, knafeh and traditional pastries to order for weddings, religious celebrations, community events and institutional supply across Michigan and beyond. Every order is different. Every order is personal. And until this project, every order was managed entirely without a screen.

The challenge — Christmas was coming, orders were getting lost, and nobody could see what was happening. The manual phone-and-paper workflow that had worked reliably for decades was buckling under seasonal volume — and the stakeholder needed management visibility before the peak hit.

The outcome — A digital operating system — a Staff dashboard (~52 screens) and an AI-driven Admin dashboard (~28 screens) on one shared design system — that captures every order digitally, makes status visible at every stage, and gives the whole team a single source of truth.

The Story

How it started:
Christmas was coming.

01 — The call

The stakeholder didn't arrive with a feature list. They arrived with a problem — and a deadline. Christmas was approaching, the highest-volume, highest-stress, most failure-prone time of their year.

02 — Forty-five years on memory

Every order, every production instruction, every delivery had been managed through phone calls, paper notes, and the memory of a small, trusted team. It worked — for decades. Until it couldn't.

03 — The breaking point

A process that reliably handled 30 orders a week broke down under 100. Not because the team worked differently — because the process had no capacity to scale. Orders fell through the gap between being recorded and being acted on.

04 — Why it mattered

This was not a redesign. There was no prior system to improve, no analytics to review. The only reference was the people — and a 45-year reputation built entirely on getting custom orders right.

02 · Problem Statement

The problem was never one task.
It was nobody knowing the state of anything.

The biggest problem was not any individual task being difficult. It was that nobody could know what state the operation was in at any given moment.

01

Orders getting lost

During peak season, orders taken by phone fell through the gap between being recorded and being acted on. A busy day meant some orders simply never made it to production.

02

No visibility across the operation

Management had no way to see the status of all active orders at once. The only method was to physically walk to each team and ask. During Christmas volume, that was impossible.

03

Too slow for peak volume

The system that reliably handled 30 orders a week broke down under 100. Not because the team changed — because the process had no capacity to scale.

04

Details getting wrong between staff

Custom specs — quantities, flavour variations, packaging, delivery instructions — were passed verbally. By the time they reached the baker, critical details were missing, assumed, or wrong.

03 · Research

Five methods. One clear picture.

With no existing system or documentation to audit, direct conversations with the stakeholder became the primary research method — reconstructing how the factory actually works, order by order, and triangulating it against the wider category.

Six findings, one direction

F1

Visibility is the core problem — not any single task.

Each team does their job competently. What fails is the gap between stages, when nobody knows what the previous team finished or what the next team needs.

F2

Custom specs degrade with every verbal retelling.

Research on bespoke manufacturing shows specifications lose meaningful detail through two or more verbal handoffs — wrong products baked and customer callbacks.

F3

Peak season is the real test.

A system that works on a quiet Tuesday but fails on December 23rd is not a solution. Every decision was filtered through maximum stress, maximum volume.

F4

Management needs overview before anything else.

The stakeholder's first priority was seeing all orders and their status simultaneously. That drove the dashboard-first, admin-first decision.

F5

The team trusts their existing behaviour.

Staff call customers to verify orders. That won't disappear because a dashboard exists. The design had to support it, not fight it.

F6

The system isn't broken — it's unscalable.

A manual workflow that ran for 45 years has real strengths. The design must formalize what works, not replace it with something foreign.

08 · Design Principles

Five rules that filtered every decision.

01

Visibility before action

The system's most important job is making information visible. Before anyone acts on an order, they must see it clearly, completely, and without searching.

Traces to F1 — visibility is the core problem
02

Preserve the original

Custom specifications must travel from the customer's words to the baker's hands without being filtered, summarised, or lost. The system carries the original — not an interpretation.

Traces to F2 — specs degrade with every handoff
03

Support existing behaviour

The tele-caller verification call has worked for 45 years. Design the system to make that call better — not to eliminate it. Earn trust by improving what the team already trusts.

Traces to F5 — the team trusts existing behaviour
04

Simple enough for Christmas

Every interaction must work at maximum stress, maximum volume, minimum patience. If a feature needs explanation, it won't be used on December 23rd.

Traces to F3 — peak season is the real test
05

Speed over polish at intake

Order entry must keep pace with a live phone conversation, not slow it down. A simple system the team will use beats a comprehensive one that slows them down.

11 · Major Design Decisions

Five decisions that shaped the build.

Each one with the challenge, the alternatives considered, why this path won, the impact, and the lesson. Expand to read the full reasoning.

Challenge

Most daily pain lived with front-line staff capturing and moving orders.

The decision

Design the staff workflow and admin visibility as one system, but prioritize the screens that touch every order — entry, status, and the shared record.

Alternatives

Build a management BI dashboard first; defer staff tooling.

Why this won

Visibility is only as good as the data feeding it. If staff don't capture orders cleanly, no admin view can be trusted.

Impact

A single order record every role reads and writes to — never a private copy.

Lesson

Foundational data capture earns the right to a beautiful dashboard.

14 · AI Feature · Auto-Fill

AI removes typing. It never removes judgement.

AI in this system has exactly one job: remove typing, never remove judgement.

Auto-Fill, not auto-submit

Paste raw text; the system proposes structured fields. Nothing is saved until a person confirms it.

Human review is mandatory

Every AI-extracted field passes through a review step. The human is the decision-maker, the AI is the assistant.

Transparency by colour

Purple is the single 'AI is acting' signal across the whole system — glow, pulse, confidence, auto-fill. You always know when the machine touched something.

Ethics for a trust business

A custom bakery cannot afford a wrong address or a misheard allergy. The design treats AI errors as expensive and builds the checkpoint in by default.

15 · Accessibility & Ethics

Trust is a design requirement, not a feature.

A first-time digital tool for a multilingual, multi-generational workforce is an ethics problem as much as a usability one.

Designed for trust

The system formalizes a 45-year reputation. It must never lose a detail a human would have remembered.

Readable under stress

High-contrast warm palette, large type, generous targets — legible on a busy factory floor on December 23rd.

Bilingual on the roadmap

Dearborn's Arab-American workforce means Arabic is a working language. Critical operational screens are slated to be bilingual (English + Arabic, RTL).

WCAG roadmap

Contrast, focus states, keyboard paths and semantic structure are the baseline; a full WCAG 2.2 AA pass is a committed next step, not a claim made today.

20 · Reflection

What I learned.

01

Design for the urgency, not the ideal.

The client came with a real deadline — Christmas. A useful tool delivered in time is worth more than a perfect tool delivered after the damage. Scope decisions made under real constraints are still design decisions; they need to be owned, not apologised for.

02

Support existing behaviour before changing it.

The tele-caller verification call has worked for 45 years. Designing the Order Summary Review to make that call better gets adoption. Trying to eliminate it gets resistance. The fastest path to trust is making the existing process undeniably better.

03

Role-based design starts with permissions, not screens.

Building the 5-role permission system into the foundation means the next phase — differentiated home screens — has real infrastructure to build on, rather than needing a retrofit.

04

First-time digitalisation is not redesign.

No prior system, no analytics, no existing mental model. The only reference is the people and how they work. Listening to what the client describes — not just what they ask for — is the entire research method.

21 · Future Roadmap

What comes next.

01

Role-based home screens

Each of the 5 roles sees a home tailored to their daily tasks. Warehouse doesn't need AR aging; admin doesn't need only a pick list.

02

Production queue for bakers

The baking team has no screen yet. What to bake today, in what quantity, with what spec — needs its own dedicated interface.

03

Automatic stage-handoff notifications

When fulfilment marks an order complete, shipping is notified instantly — the digital version of the verbal handoff.

04

Mobile & tablet view

Factory-floor workers aren't at desks. Fulfilment and shipping need touch-friendly, readable-in-production interfaces.

05

Bilingual UI — English + Arabic

Dearborn's Arab-American workforce means Arabic is a working language. Critical operational screens should be bilingual, RTL-aware.

06

Analytics & inventory intelligence

Reorder automation, demand forecasting for peak season, and production planning built on real order history.
